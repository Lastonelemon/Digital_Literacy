In today's digital world, how we communicate online says a lot about us — especially in academic or professional settings. Poor communication doesn't just cause confusion; it can quietly damage how others see you before you've even had a chance to make a real impression.
Take something as simple as a student emailing a professor: "Hey, can't submit, extend pls." It's easy to dash off a message like that without thinking twice, but from the professor's end, it reads as careless — and honestly, it probably gets ignored. Not out of cruelty, but because it doesn't give them much reason to take the request seriously.
That contrast is what makes email etiquette worth paying attention to. A clear subject line, a respectful greeting, and a brief but honest explanation — these aren't just formalities. They signal that you value the other person's time and that your request deserves a genuine response.
What this really comes down to is intentionality. When you slow down and think about how you're saying something, not just what you're saying, the whole tone of the conversation shifts. People respond differently when they feel respected.
Working through this made me realize that digital communication isn't just a skill — it's a habit. And like most habits, the small choices you make consistently end up shaping how the world sees you.
